The Maker

Jitendra knows this art form is threatened. Digital tools can now replicate in seconds what took him weeks. He knows this and keeps going anyway — because he believes there will always be people who understand the difference between something made by a hand that gave everything, and something generated by a machine that gave nothing. Those people always find him.

Shishya Parampara

A tradition passed from guru to shishya
for over seventy years.
Not through blood. Through devotion.

An unnamed court painter of Mewar passed this craft to Kishan Singh Solanki before India's independence. Kishan passed it to Naresh Yadav. Naresh passed it to Jitendra. Jitendra now teaches Vineet Yadav. That knowledge — every stroke, every pigment, every proportion — carried forward by hand, eye, and complete surrender to the practice.

Jitendra now teaches his own students. The line continues.

The Tools

The paints, the brushes, the instruments of a lifetime.

Each colour is a mineral. Lapis from Afghanistan. Malachite from Central Asia. Ochre from the earth nearby. Ground by hand in stone mortars — the same method used in Mughal ateliers four hundred years ago.

The stones are ground fresh each session. Mixed with natural binders — gum arabic, sometimes a drop of ox gall. No preservatives. No consistency across batches. That variation is part of what makes each painting irreproducible.

The brushes are made from squirrel tail hair — specifically the Siberian red squirrel, whose fur has a natural taper and spring no synthetic fibre can replicate. Each brush is hand-bound. The finest hold a single hair at the tip. You cannot buy these in a shop.

Pigments on the table, finished works on the wall. The studio holds decades of practice in one frame.

Every corner of the studio holds pigment, memory, and work. Nothing is ornamental. Everything is used.
