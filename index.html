The Painting

A scene painted a hundred times
across four generations.
Never exactly the same.

Udaipur ki Shaam depicts the Maharana at rest in his lakeside pavilion, watching the sun set behind the Lake Palace. It is one of the oldest subjects in Mewar miniature tradition — and one of the most demanding. The water, the sky, the gold, the lotuses — each element requires a different technique, a different pigment, a different kind of patience.

The Lotus

47 petals.
Each painted one by one.

In Mewar miniature tradition, the lotus is not decoration — it is symbol. Purity rising from still water. Grace earned through patience. Each petal in this painting was outlined first, then filled with three translucent washes of pink, then highlighted at the tip. The smallest petal is 4mm wide. It took twenty minutes.

The Lake Palace

Every window lit from within.

The Lake Palace as painted here is 7cm wide in the original work. Within that 7cm, Ramesh has painted every arch, every dome, every balustrade — and the gold light glowing from each window. The reflection in the water below is not a mirror — it is repainted entirely, with slightly different brushstrokes to suggest movement. This single element took three days.

The Maharana

Gold leaf thinner
than a human eyelash.

The turban ornament, the pearl necklace, the embroidery on the robe — all applied in 24-karat gold leaf under a magnifying lens. The individual pearls are 1.5mm wide. The eyelashes were painted with a brush containing a single hair. This is a face that took four days to complete.

The Making

This is what the painting
looked like before
it became what it is.

Every piece begins as blank handmade paper and becomes, through weeks of uninterrupted work, what you are looking at now. These are the stages — in sequence, in real time.

Day 1 – 3
01
The Ground

Handmade wasli paper, prepared with chalk and gum arabic. Sanded between each coat until the surface is perfectly smooth. This step is completely invisible in the finished work. That is the point — the foundation must be flawless precisely because no one will ever see it.

3 Days of preparation. Zero visible result.
Day 4 – 7
02
The Outline

The entire composition drawn freehand with a single-hair brush and ink. No pencil. No ruler. No guide. Every proportion, every figure, every architectural detail held entirely in memory — a memory built across three generations of practice. A single wrong line cannot be corrected. It must be started again.

4 Days. One continuous line.
Day 8 – 12
03
First Colour

Thin translucent washes of natural pigment — lapis lazuli for the water, ochre for the architecture, pale malachite for the foliage. Nothing is opaque yet. The paper still shows through. These first washes are not the colour you see in the finished work — they are the colour beneath the colour. The base that gives everything else its depth.

5 Days. The painting begins to breathe.
Day 13 – 17
04
Deep Pigment

Layer after layer after layer. Each one dried before the next was applied. The deep lapis blue of the water required fourteen separate coats. The vermillion of the robes, eleven. The sky — its gradient from deep blue to burnt orange — was built up across eight days of careful, patient layering. No gold yet. This is the painting at 80%.

9 Days. Fourteen layers of blue alone.

"The brush has one hair. The hand does not shake. This is not talent — it is 34 years."

Day 18 – 21
05
Gold & Completion

24-karat gold leaf, applied under magnification. The borders, the jewellery, the window light of the Lake Palace — all added in the final four days. Then the eyelashes. The pearl beads. The last details so small they are invisible to the naked eye until you look for them. And then you cannot stop looking.

21 Days total. ₹850 per day.
